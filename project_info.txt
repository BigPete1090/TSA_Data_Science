Tourism Data Science Project Summary

Dataset:
- Name: Hotel Booking Demand Dataset
- File: data/hotel_bookings.csv
- Format: CSV
- Original article: Antonio, N., de Almeida, A., & Nunes, L. (2019). Hotel booking demand datasets. Data in Brief, 22, 41-49. DOI: 10.1016/j.dib.2018.11.126
- Download mirror used in this workspace: https://raw.githubusercontent.com/salves94/hotel-exploratory-data-analysis/master/hotel_bookings.csv

Research questions:
1. Which hotel booking factors are most associated with cancellation?
2. Can machine learning predict booking cancellation accurately from booking-time tourism variables?

Analysis section for presentation:
- Compared cancellation rates between City Hotels and Resort Hotels.
- Compared cancellation rates across deposit types to see whether payment commitment affects booking behavior.
- Compared customer types such as Transient, Contract, Group, and Transient-Party.
- Compared bookings with short vs long lead times using lead-time quartiles.
- Compared bookings with different numbers of special requests.
- Compared numeric tourism and booking variables using a correlation matrix.
- Compared three machine learning models: Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, and Random Forest.

Interesting descriptive results:
- Overall cancellation rate was 37.04%, so cancellations are common enough to be an important tourism business problem.
- City Hotels had a much higher cancellation rate than Resort Hotels: 41.73% vs 27.76%.
- Non-Refund bookings had the highest cancellation rate at 99.36%, while No Deposit bookings were much lower at 28.38%.
- Transient customers had the highest cancellation rate at 40.75%, while Group customers were much lower at 10.23%.
- Longer lead times were strongly linked with more cancellations:
  0 to 18 days: 14.64%
  19 to 69 days: 35.86%
  70 to 160 days: 42.58%
  161 to 737 days: 55.44%
- Bookings with no special requests canceled much more often than bookings with several requests:
  0 requests: 47.72%
  1 request: 22.02%
  2 requests: 22.10%
  3 requests: 17.86%
  4 requests: 10.59%
  5 requests: 5.00%

Interesting correlation results:
- The strongest positive non-target correlation with cancellation was lead_time at 0.2931.
- Previous cancellations were also positively related to cancellation at 0.1101.
- The strongest negative relationships were total_of_special_requests at -0.2347 and required_car_parking_spaces at -0.1955.
- This means bookings that look more committed or customized were less likely to be canceled.

Interesting machine learning results:
- Random Forest was the best model.
- Random Forest performance:
  Accuracy: 0.8713
  Precision: 0.8224
  Recall: 0.8324
  F1: 0.8274
  ROC AUC: 0.9451
- Logistic Regression was still strong, but weaker than Random Forest.
- Decision Tree had the highest recall among the weaker models, which means it caught many cancellations but made more false positives.
- The top Random Forest features included deposit type, lead time, number of special requests, parking spaces, ADR, previous cancellations, and customer type.

What these results mean:
- Cancellation in tourism bookings is not random.
- Customer commitment signals matter a lot.
- Earlier bookings are more likely to be canceled.
- More customized or committed bookings, such as those with special requests or parking needs, are less likely to be canceled.
- This makes the project more meaningful than a simple rating-prediction project because it connects directly to hotel operations, tourism planning, and revenue management.
